Welcome to the Great Smoky Mountains National Park Wildlife Checklist! This guide is designed to help visitors discover and appreciate the rich diversity of wildlife that inhabits the park. From the majestic black bears and elusive bobcats to the vibrant bird species and unique amphibians, this checklist allows you to track your sightings and learn more about the park's natural inhabitants. Whether you're hiking through dense forests or exploring the park's stunning landscapes, the checklist offers an engaging way to connect with the park's ecosystems and enhance your wildlife experience. Get ready to embark on an unforgettable adventure while observing the incredible creatures that call this protected area home.

American Black Bear

  • "Ursus americanus"
  • Mammal
  • National Park

The American Black Bear is a common sight in the Great Smoky Mountains, known for its black to brown fur. Omnivorous and adaptable, these bears eat a variety of foods, including fruits, nuts, and small mammals. Skilled climbers and swimmers, black bears typically hibernate in winter. While large and powerful, they are generally shy and avoid human interaction, playing a crucial role in the park's ecosystem.

Source: (Images courtesy of NPS)

Barred Owl

  • "Strix varia"
  • Bird
  • National Park

The Barred Owl is a striking bird found in the Great Smoky Mountains, recognized by its brown and white striped plumage and distinctive "who-cooks-for-you" call. These nocturnal hunters thrive in wooded areas, preying on small mammals, birds, and amphibians. With excellent vision and hearing, barred owls are skilled at silently hunting at night. Typically elusive, they play a key role in controlling rodent populations in the park's ecosystem.

Source: ( © Blair Dudeck)

Coyote

  • "Canas latrans"
  • Mammal
  • National Park

The coyote is a versatile and adaptable predator found in the Great Smoky Mountains. With a slender build and a bushy tail, coyotes are often seen in forests, meadows, and along park roads. They are omnivores, feeding on small mammals, birds, fruits, and insects. Coyotes are known for their keen senses and remarkable ability to survive in a variety of habitats. Though typically shy and elusive, they contribute to maintaining balance in the park’s ecosystem by controlling populations of smaller animals.

Source: (https://greatsmokyvacations.wordpress.com/2022/01/15/coyotes-in-east-tennessee/)

Eastern Box Turtle

  • "Terrapene carolina carolina"
  • Reptile
  • National Park

The Eastern Box Turtle is a small, colorful turtle commonly found in the Great Smoky Mountains. Known for its high-domed shell, which features intricate patterns of yellow and orange, this terrestrial turtle is often spotted in wooded areas and along streams. Box turtles are omnivores, feeding on plants, insects, and small invertebrates. They are unique for their ability to completely close their shells, providing protection from predators. Slow-moving and long-lived, they play an important role in the park’s ecosystem by helping to disperse seeds and maintain soil health.

Source: (Wikipedia)

Elk

  • "Cervus canadensis"
  • Mammal
  • National Park

The Bull Elk is a majestic animal found in the Great Smoky Mountains, known for its impressive size and large, antlered rack. During the fall rut, bull elk are particularly notable for their loud bugling calls as they compete for mates. These herbivores graze on grasses, shrubs, and tree bark, and can often be seen in open meadows or along the park's edges. Once near extinction in the region, elk are now a symbol of wildlife conservation efforts, playing a key role in shaping the park's ecosystems through their grazing habits.

Source: (https://www.romanticasheville.com/elk.htm)

Gray Squirrel (Eastern)

  • "Sciurus carolinensis"
  • Rodent
  • National Park

The Gray Squirrel is a common and lively sight in the Great Smoky Mountains, easily recognized by its bushy tail and gray fur. These agile rodents thrive in wooded areas, where they feed on nuts, seeds, and fruits, often storing food for the winter months. Known for their playful behavior, gray squirrels are excellent climbers and can be seen darting through trees or foraging on the forest floor. They play a vital role in seed dispersal, helping to maintain the health of the park’s diverse plant life.

Source: (Images courtesy of NPS)

Spotted Salamander

  • "Ambystoma maculatum"
  • Amphibian
  • National Park

The Spotted Salamander is a striking amphibian found in the Great Smoky Mountains, easily identified by its black body adorned with bright yellow spots. Preferring moist, wooded habitats, they are most often seen during their breeding season in early spring, when they migrate to vernal pools to lay eggs. These nocturnal creatures feed on insects, worms, and other small invertebrates. The spotted salamander plays an important role in controlling insect populations and maintaining the health of the park’s ecosystem.

Source: (Lang Elliot)

White-tailed Deer

  • "Odocoileus virginianus"
  • Mammal
  • National Park

The White-tailed Deer (Odocoileus virginianus) is a common and graceful sight in the Great Smoky Mountains. Recognizable by its white underside and tail, which it raises as a warning signal when startled, this deer is a herbivore, feeding on grasses, shrubs, and tree leaves. Active primarily at dawn and dusk, white-tailed deer are known for their agility and speed, helping them evade predators. As a key species in the park, they help shape plant communities through browsing, and their presence contributes to the overall health of the ecosystem.
